01 — Abstract

The Mission

Egypt's development is tackled by several grand challenges. This project addresses improving the use of alternative energies and reducing and adapting to the effects of climate change. With less than 5% usage of renewable and alternative sources, Egypt's reliance on fossil fuels leads to increased greenhouse gas emissions and a rising annual average temperature of 0.4°C per year.

The specific problem addressed is the inadequacy of electrical infrastructure and heavy dependence on diesel generators in Ras-Banas, Red Sea Governorate — causing difficulty in all aspects of life for its residents.

The solution is an Archimedes Spiral Wind Turbine (ASWT) enhanced by adding a wind lens, dramatically increasing efficiency by 80%. The prototype generated 221.7 J in 5 minutes — exceeding the 150 J design requirement by over 40%.

02 — Introduction & Problem

The Energy Crisis

Ras-Banas peninsula, located south of Marsa Alam in the Red Sea Governorate (23.9667°N, 35.5000°E), suffers from weak infrastructure that prevents electricity import from neighboring governorates. The area has a measured average wind speed of 6.23 m/s — ideal for wind turbine deployment. Prior solutions studied include the Gabal El-Zeit Wind Farm (efficient but requires high start speed) and Aswan's High Dam (stable flow but requires significant hydraulic head). The ASWT overcomes both limitations.

03 — Materials & Design

The Prototype

Key Components

01
Archimedes Spiral Blades (3×)
Three spiral blades cut from polyethylene (PE) plastic buckets, shaped using a 3D-printed blade frame. Blades were heat-flattened then glued to the PLA+ 3D-printed shaft. Turbine diameter: 18 cm.
02
Wind Lens (Aluminum)
Multi-section aluminum wind lens comprising a flange, converging concentrator, cylindrical connecting section, and diverging diffuser. Cut by laser cutter, bolted and screwed together. Accelerates inlet wind to ~7.5 m/s.
03
3D-Printed Shaft & Supports
PLA+ 3D-printed shaft (in two segments joined by a mortise-and-tenon joint), turbine holders, and blade frame. A 6004-2rs ball bearing reduces friction at the front of the shaft.
04
300 RPM DC Motor & Base
Motor shaft inserted into the rear of the turbine shaft to generate electricity. Plywood supports hold both the ASWT and wind lens assembly, with all components fixed using M6 hex bolts.

🔧

One blade detached from the shaft during construction. Re-attached with MDF adhesive kit. Motor shaft couldn't fit — solved by carving with soldering iron.
💥
Blade Breakage
A blade broke during high-wind testing. Re-attached using two-part epoxy adhesive. Prototype subsequently remained intact and functionally stable.
05 — Analysis

The Science

The Archimedes Spiral Wind Turbine (ASWT) is an innovative Horizontal Axis Wind Turbine whose blades follow the Archimedes Spiral curve — also known as the Arithmetic Spiral. This spiral has the property that any ray from the origin intersects successive turnings at a constant separation distance.

r = θ
Archimedes Spiral in Polar Coordinates — where r is the radial distance and θ is the angle

Unlike standard HAWTs (which use only lift) or VAWTs (which use only drag), the ASWT uses both lift and drag forces simultaneously (Kim et al., 2014). This makes standard Blade Element Momentum (BEM) theory inapplicable for performance prediction. Instead, a Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) model using Ansys Fluent was used to solve the Navier-Stokes equations via a meshing process — computing aerodynamic performance in millions of small cells. The efficiency value of Cp = 0.293 was extracted from Jang et al. (2019).

The wind lens was first introduced for wind turbines by Yuji Ohya of Kyushu University. The ASWT wind lens contains: a vertical flange, a converging concentrator, a cylindrical connecting section, and a diverging diffuser. It operates on three principles:

A₁V₁ = A₂V₂
Continuity Equation — increasing wind velocity as the concentrator cross-section area decreases
P₁ + ½ρv₁² + ρgh₁ = P₂ + ½ρv₂² + ρgh₂
Bernoulli's Equation — increased velocity causes decreased static pressure, drawing more wind in

The diverging diffuser creates a high-pressure zone behind the lens. Combined with the low-pressure wake area, this creates a pressure drop that expands the mass flow rate. The flange causes air to decelerate and form high-velocity vortices near the inlet, creating low pressure that draws additional airflow — resulting in an 80% enhancement in energy output relative to the bare ASWT.

The Tip Speed Ratio (TSR) describes the relationship between the tangential blade speed and the wind speed facing the turbine's swept area. The ASWT achieves peak power coefficient (Cp) when TSR is between 1.5 and 2.5.

λ = ωR / v
TSR equation — λ: tip speed ratio, ω: angular velocity, R: radius (0.17 m), v: wind speed

At 5 m/s wind speed with ω = 35 rad/s, TSR = 1.7 — within the optimal range, meaning the prototype achieves high Cp at ~3.5 m/s wind speed. The mechanical torque was calculated as:

T = P / (Cp_motor · ω)
Torque equation — at ω = 30 rad/s, P = 0.739 W, η = 70%, T = 0.0302 N·m

This torque gives the prototype a low cut-in speed of ~2 m/s, ensuring electricity generation in low-wind conditions typical of the Ras-Banas area.

Wind power is calculated from the kinetic energy of moving air passing through the rotor swept area. Air density ρ = 1.22 kg/m³.

Pw = ½ρAv³
Wind Power equation — at test conditions, Pw = 3.558 W
η = P_output / P_w
System Efficiency — before enhancement: 0.208; after enhancement: 0.38

The twisted Archimedes spiral blade design increases torque at low wind speeds, while the wind lens amplifies the inlet wind velocity — together maximizing energy conversion. The total system efficiency nearly doubled from 20.8% to 38% after adding the wind lens. Energy losses occur primarily in the DC motor conversion (70% efficiency) and blade aerodynamic drag at off-optimal TSR values.

06 — Conclusions & Recommendations

Looking Forward

The project successfully solved the stated problem and exceeded all design requirements. The ASWT with wind lens produced 221.7 J in 5 minutes — 47.8% above the 150 J requirement — with an 80% efficiency improvement from the wind lens alone. The prototype operates from 2 m/s wind speeds, accepts wind from any direction, and produces low noise, making it ideal for Ras-Banas and similar urban/coastal settings.

🏗️
Real-life Scaling
Scale ratio 1:5 → length 0.75–3.75 m, width 0.72–3.6 m, height 0.42–2.1 m. Shaft and frame in stainless steel; spherical roller bearings model 22220; PMG-type generator; epoxy anti-corrosion coating.
✈️
Carbon Fiber Blades
Carbon fiber offers tensile strength of 3,500–7,000 MPa, stiffness of 279 GPa, and resists solar heat up to 3,600 K. Its low density reduces rotor inertia, enabling even lower cut-in speeds. UV-resistant coating standard.
🔋
Flywheel Energy Storage
Flywheel systems store 80–90% of input energy and respond in 4 milliseconds — stabilizing output when wind speed fluctuates. Ideal companion for the ASWT's variable wind environment in Ras-Banas.
